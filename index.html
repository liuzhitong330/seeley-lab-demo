Motivation Epicenter test Across disorders Relevance

The lab's central question is selective vulnerability: why does each neurodegenerative disease attack a particular set of brain regions and leave others untouched. A large part of the answer, from this lab's own work, is that diseases do not hit random regions. They target connected networks. The idea is that pathology starts at an epicenter and spreads to the regions wired to it, so a region's fate depends on how it is connected rather than where it sits.

That gives a clean prediction I could test on real data. If the network idea is right, the regions most strongly connected to a disease epicenter should be the ones that lose the most tissue. Below I take real cortical atrophy maps for a set of disorders and a healthy brain wiring map, place each disorder's epicenter, and check whether connectivity to it tracks atrophy.

How the test works

The atrophy maps are group cortical-thinning measures across 68 regions from the ENIGMA consortium, one map per disorder. The wiring map is a healthy connectome from the Human Connectome Project, measured either as functional coupling or structural fibers. For each disorder I define the epicenter as the single region with the most thinning, then, across all other regions, I compare their connectivity to that epicenter against how much they have thinned. A negative relationship means better-connected regions are more atrophied, which is what the network idea predicts.

The relationship is negative for almost every disorder, meaning the better-connected a region is to the epicenter, the more it has thinned. It is strongest for right temporal lobe epilepsy, bipolar disorder, OCD, and Parkinson's disease. A couple of conditions sit near zero, which is a useful reminder that connectivity is one ingredient and not the whole story. That fits where the field has gone next, adding local molecular vulnerability on top of the network to explain what connectivity alone misses.

Why this matters for the lab

This is the lab's own network-degeneration idea, run as a quick reproducible check on open data. The same setup is the seed of a prediction tool: given where a patient's disease starts, connectivity ranks which regions are likely to be hit next, which is exactly the kind of monitoring the lab wants for progranulin and C9orf72 carriers before symptoms appear. It also extends naturally to the lab's cellular work, by asking which regions are both well-connected to the epicenter and molecularly primed to be vulnerable.

Methods note

Data from Hansen et al. 2022, Nature Communications, and its public repository: ENIGMA cortical-thickness case-control maps for 13 disorders, and HCP functional and structural group connectomes, all in the Cammoun scale-033 parcellation of 68 cortical regions. Region positions are a two-dimensional layout from the real inter-region distance matrix. Epicenter is the region of greatest thinning; the statistic is the Pearson correlation between each region's connectivity to the epicenter and its cortical thickness change. This is group-level, cortical, and correlational. ENIGMA does not include FTD, so Parkinson's stands in as the neurodegenerative case here, and the lab's own studies use patient-specific epicenters and longitudinal follow-up, which this simple version does not.
